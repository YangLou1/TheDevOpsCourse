Kanban: Work Is a Flow, Not a Stock

Too often, people come to believe they are productive because they are doing lots of things. If you asked them for evidence of their productivity, they would show you all of the tasks "on their plate," i.e., their to-do list.

This brings us to the distinction in economics between "stocks" and "flows." Certain things in economics, such as the number of factories a business owns, or the amount of capital an investor has on hand, are a stock: what we are looking to measure here is an amount that exists at a certain point in time. But other economic phenomena are better understood as a flow: e.g., our companies factories can produce 100 automobiles per hour, or an investor's capital produces a 5% return per year.

The point of kanban boards is to emphasize that one's productivity is a flow, and not a stock. One is productive to the extent that one moves products further down the value stream, and not to the extent that one has stacked up lots of unfinished projects at one's work station.
